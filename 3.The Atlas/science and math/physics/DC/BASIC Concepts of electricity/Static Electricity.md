## 1. Historical Observations of Electrostatic Forces

- **Early Discoveries:** Centuries ago, it was observed that certain materials physically attract or repel one another after being rubbed together.
    
    - **Glass + Silk:** Rubbing glass with silk causes them to attract and stick together.
        
    - **Wax + Wool:** Rubbing paraffin wax with wool cloth similarly creates an attractive force.
        
- **The Rule of Identity (Repulsion):** Identical materials treated with their respective cloths always **repel** each other (e.g., rubbed glass repels rubbed glass; rubbed wax repels rubbed wax).
    
- **Cross-Material Interaction:** A piece of glass rubbed with silk will **attract** a piece of wax rubbed with wool.
    
- **The Two Categories:** All materials displaying these properties fall strictly into two groups:
    
    1. Attracted to glass and repelled by wax.
        
    2. Repelled by glass and attracted to wax.
        
    
    - _Note:_ No material reacts to one without reacting to the other, or is attracted/repelled by both. The cloths used for rubbing also experience repulsion against each other (e.g., silk repels silk, wool repels wool).
        

## 2. Early Theories: Fluids and Charges

- **Charles Dufay:** Demonstrated that rubbing produces two distinct types of physical changes, evidenced by the two opposing forces: attraction and repulsion.
    
- **Benjamin Franklin’s Single-Fluid Theory:** Postulated that electricity is a single invisible "fluid" exchanged during friction.
    
    - **Excess (+):** An object with a surplus of fluid is **positively** charged.
        
    - **Deficiency (-):** An object with a shortage of fluid is **negatively** charged.
        
    - **Nomenclature Origin:** Franklin guessed wool rubbed fluid _off_ the wax, designating **wax as negative** and **wool as positive**. (Modern physics later revealed the fluid—electrons—actually moves from the wool _to_ the wax, meaning electrons carry a negative charging influence).
        
- **Conservation of Charge:** Total charge is conserved; the two materials rubbed against each other always develop opposite and equal categories of charge.
    

## 3. Quantitative Measurement (Coulomb’s Law)

- **Charles Coulomb (1780s):** Used a _torsional balance_ to measure the exact force generated between charged objects.
    
- **The Coulomb (C):** The standard unit of electrical charge named in his honor.
    
    - **Force Scale:** Two point charges of $1\text{ C}$ placed $1\text{ meter}$ apart generate a force of approximately **$9\text{ billion newtons}$** ($\approx 2\text{ billion pounds}$).
        
    - **Elementary Charge:** One electron holds a charge of roughly $0.00000000000000000016\text{ coulombs}$ ($1.6 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$).
        
    - Conversely, $1\text{ coulomb}$ represents an excess or deficiency of about **$6.25 \times 10^{18}$ electrons**.
        

## 4. The Composition of the Atom

All matter is made of atoms, which consist of three primary fundamental particles:

1. **Protons:** Positive (+) charge, located tightly bound in the nucleus. The number of protons defines the elemental identity of the atom (e.g., Carbon has 6 protons).
    
2. **Neutrons:** Neutral (no charge), located in the nucleus. Adding or removing neutrons changes the atom's mass or stability (creating isotopes or radioactivity) but does not alter its chemical identity.
    
3. **Electrons:** Negative (-) charge, occupy the vast empty space surrounding the nucleus.
    

- _Exception:_ The **protium isotope of hydrogen ($^1\text{H}_1$)** is the only naturally occurring atom that lacks neutrons, containing only 1 proton and 1 electron.
    
- **The Strong Nuclear Force:** Holds the positively charged protons together in the nucleus over incredibly short distances, overcoming their natural electrostatic repulsion.
    
- **Net Neutrality:** In a normal atom, the number of protons equals the number of electrons, rendering the net electrical charge zero.
    

## 5. Mechanism and Applications of Static Electricity

- **Mechanism:** Electrons have significantly more freedom than nuclear particles. Mechanical friction (rubbing) supplies enough energy to dislodge electrons from the atoms of one material and transfer them to another.
    
- **Why it is "Static":** Displaced electrons tend to remain stationary (static) after moving because they are typically transferred between _insulating_ materials.
    
- **Practical Engineering Realities:**
    
    - **Nuisance & Hazards:** Static electricity can ignite volatile materials like gunpowder (graphite is added to black/smokeless powder to safely dissipate charge) and easily destroys sensitive semiconductor circuits.
        
    - **Economic Limits:** While electrostatic forces can run specialized high-voltage, low-current motors, it is not economically viable for large-scale power.
        
    - **Useful Applications:** Industrial applications include **xerographic printing (photocopying)**, **electrostatic air filters**, and **Van de Graaff generators**.
